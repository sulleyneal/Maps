BROKEN BOW

Pines, clear water & cabin country
at the foot of the Ouachita Mountains

❧

Broken Bow grew up around the Dierks brothers’ sawmills in 1911 — lumbermen who named the new town for their old home of Broken Bow, Nebraska — in the timberlands of the Choctaw Nation. North of town the Mountain Fork was dammed in 1968, and behind it rose Broken Bow Lake: fourteen thousand acres of famously clear water walled in by pine bluffs.

Beavers Bend State Park, laid out by the CCC in the 1930s where the river bends through a rock canyon, anchors it all. Around it has boomed Hochatown — cabins, breweries and outfitters strung along US 259 — while below the dam the river runs cold enough for year-round trout.

    On Foot

    The park’s trails knit into the 12-mile David Boren network: easy river walks (Beaver Lodge, Cedar Bluff, each ≈1 mi), the favorite Friends Trail loop (1.5 mi to a ridge over the lake) and the strenuous Skyline Trail (≈5 mi, creek crossings, allow 4+ hours). Trails draw as green dashed lines as you zoom in; named ones are labeled.

    Behind the Wheel

    Hochatown’s outfitters rent street-legal UTVs and run guided rides over 75+ miles of Ouachita National Forest roads north and west of the lake — rock, mud and ridgeline views. The fabled K-Trail rides the Kiamichi crest about an hour north. A caution: the Three Rivers and Honobia Creek WMAs beyond the forest are not open riding — they require paid land-access permits and allow ATVs only for deer hunters in season.

    Late July, Truthfully

    Expect mid-90s and humid. Hike at first light, be off the Skyline by late morning, then get in the water: the lake runs warm and clear, while the river below the dam stays cold enough that waders fish for trout all summer — brisk and glorious on hot afternoons. The swim beach on the Mountain Fork inside the park is the classic cool-off. It is peak season: book cabins, UTVs and zipline slots ahead.
